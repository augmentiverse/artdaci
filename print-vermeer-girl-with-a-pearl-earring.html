The Dutch Golden Age · Spread 004

Girl with a Pearl Earring

Johannes Vermeer

c. 1665Oil on canvas44.5 × 39 cmMauritshuis

A face turning through light

Not a named portrait but a tronie, this concentrated study of expression, costume, and light transforms an unknown model into an unforgettable encounter.

Historical Context

Painted in Delft around 1665, the work belongs to the Dutch tradition of the tronie: an imaginative character study rather than a commissioned likeness. The fanciful turban and dark setting remove the girl from ordinary time and place.

The Encounter

Her body turns away while her head returns toward us. Direct eye contact and parted lips make the instant feel spontaneous, yet the identity of the sitter and the meaning of her expression remain unresolved.

Color and Light

Costly natural ultramarine gives the blue turban exceptional depth. Yellow cloth, luminous flesh, a white collar, and a few wet highlights emerge from darkness through alternating opaque paint and thin glazes.

The Pearl

The earring is an optical performance made from only a few strokes. A bright highlight, a softer reflection below, and the surrounding darkness make it seem spherical, even though no hook is visible.

Hidden Background

The ground now appears nearly black. Technical imaging found that Vermeer originally painted a translucent green curtain behind the figure; changes in pigments and glaze caused its folds to fade from view.

Look Closely

Vermeer builds the encounter from a handful of precise optical events. Move from gaze to pearl, then notice what time has changed.

  1. 1The gaze. Eye contact arrives at the instant the head turns, creating intimacy without explanation.
  2. 2The earring. No contour or hook is visible; highlights and surrounding darkness make the eye invent a sphere.
  3. 3The ultramarine turban. Costly lapis-lazuli blue gives the imaginative costume extraordinary depth.
  4. 4The parted lips. Soft edges and wet highlights make a silent image feel about to speak.
  5. 5The lost green curtain. Scientific imaging revealed folds in a background that now appears almost black.

An Unknown Sitter

No identification is proven. Suggestions include Vermeer's daughter Maria, his patron's daughter Magdalena van Ruijven, or an allegorical figure such as a Sibyl. Griet, the maid made famous by Tracy Chevalier's novel and its film adaptation, is fictional. The exotic headcloth and costume support the work's classification as a tronie, not a documentary portrait.

Materials and Method

Vermeer worked over a yellowish-white ground of chalk, lead white, and ochre, first mapping light and shadow in a dark monochrome underlayer. Natural ultramarine made from lapis lazuli gives the headscarf its intense blue. Opaque passages, translucent glazes, tiny dots, and softened edges build the illusion of skin, cloth, moisture, and reflected light.

Optics and the Earring

The earring has no contour or visible hook: a few highlights make the eye complete its form. Its scale and metallic reflection have led some researchers to propose polished tin rather than a pearl. Vermeer's selective focus and geometric control also invite camera-obscura theories, possibly involving a booth-sized device, but no document or physical trace proves that he owned or used one.

Research and Conservation

Bought in 1881 for only two guilders and thirty cents, the neglected painting entered the Mauritshuis collection in 1902. A public restoration in 1994 clarified the green background and subtle gaze. In 2018, The Girl in the Spotlight used non-invasive microscopy, MA-XRF, and reflectance imaging to map pigments, eyelashes, paint layers, degradation products, and the faded curtain without taking samples from the surface.
